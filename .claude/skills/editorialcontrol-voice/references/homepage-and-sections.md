# Reference: homepage, sections, About, Contact

Actual copy from editorialcontrol.org. Read these when writing short-form for the site.

## Masthead / issue stamp

The homepage carries a persistent issue stamp. This is brand, not metadata — present it on every rendered page as part of the identity:

> Vol. 01 / Issue 00 · Pre-press · Sunday, April 19, 2026

Format: `Vol. [##] / Issue [##] · [stage] · [full date]`
Stages the site uses: `Pre-press`, `In press`, `Published`. You can invent new stages (`Proof`, `Ready to ship`) when they fit.

## "Editorial spread" section

The homepage next has a Q&A-structured section titled "Editorial spread," with three short columns:

> **What's here**
>
> Posts on getting real work done with LLM agents. Not tutorials. Not benchmarks. The
> day-to-day texture of running workflows where the model is a collaborator and the
> human's job is to notice when the work goes sideways.
>
> **Why bother**
>
> Because the interesting part isn't the model — it's what it does to your process.
> Calendars, review cycles, publishing cadences: all of it shifts when the person at
> the desk can delegate, then correct. That shift deserves a press of its own.
>
> **How we publish**
>
> Slowly. Every piece here is drafted by an agent and finished by a human, using the
> same editorial calendar you can read about in the posts. Nothing ships that wouldn't
> survive a read-aloud. The receipts stay on the page.

Notes:
- Labels are bolded, not headed.
- Each answer is 2–3 sentences, ~35 words.
- The third answer ("How we publish") has the signature line "The receipts stay on the page." That line is a house motto — reachable for reuse.

## "On the desk" section

A numbered list of the publication's standing sections — the topics it expects to return to, not individual posts.

> ## On the desk
>
> The running list of sections this publication is working on. Each one is a place we
> expect to return to, not a one-shot post.
>
> ---
>
> 1. § 01
>
>    ### Workflow patterns
>
>    What it looks like when agents do the grunt work and humans review. Templates, branches, review loops.
>
> 2. § 02
>
>    ### Editorial cadence
>
>    Shipping writing on a schedule. Calendars, drafts, review, publication — run by agents, finished by a human.
>
> 3. § 03
>
>    ### Automation that actually ships
>
>    The difference between a cute demo and something you use every week without noticing.

Pattern:
- Numbered list (1., 2., 3.).
- Each entry: `§ [##]` marker, then an H3 section name, then one sentence.
- Section names are sentence-case and short (2–4 words preferred).
- The one-sentence description states the distinction the section exists to cover, not a summary of future posts.

## About page and Contact page cues

We don't have explicit About copy to reproduce, but the site's voice suggests the About page would:
- Be written by Orion in first person.
- Mention audiocontrol.org as the practical project where the workflows described on editorialcontrol actually run.
- Describe the publication's cadence and scope honestly ("slowly," "small," "pre-press").
- Avoid credentialing with a résumé list. Name the work, not the titles.
- Probably include the colophon-adjacent line: "Published from a terminal."

Contact page should be: a mailto link styled as "Write to the editor →" and one or two sentences noting that replies happen, eventually, and that the editor would rather be working than doing inbox. The voice stays the voice.

## House phrases — on-brand and reusable

These have appeared on the site and can be echoed, or reached for as sign-offs:

- "Published from a terminal."
- "The receipts stay on the page."
- "No form, no funnel."
- "Slowly published."

Use them where they fit. Don't use more than one per piece — they're garnish, not filling.
